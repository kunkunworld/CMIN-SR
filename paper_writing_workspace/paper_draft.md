# Title

**Validity-Aware Spectral Diagnostics for Multifractal Random Walk Inference Under Finite Samples**

# Abstract

Multiscale fluctuations in complex stochastic systems often exhibit heavy tails,
intermittency, and nonlinear scaling spectra, features that are commonly
interpreted as evidence of multifractality. In finite observations, however,
this interpretation is ambiguous: monofractal processes can display stable
scaling, heavy-tailed or regime-switching signals can generate apparent
curvature, and empirical high-order moments may be strongly affected by sample
length and scale range. We propose CMIN-SR, a validity-aware spectral diagnostic
framework for assessing multifractal random walk (MRW)-like evidence under
finite samples. The framework estimates an empirical scaling spectrum, projects
it onto competing monofractal and MRW spectral families, and reports calibrated
diagnostic scores for scaling stability, nonlinear curvature, monofractal
compatibility, MRW compatibility, boundary behavior, and instability. In
controlled analytic spectrum space, the spectral calibrator separates linear
monofractal, boundary MRW, and strongly curved MRW spectra, with representative
MRW compatibility increasing from approximately \(0.13\) for linear spectra to
\(0.94\) for curved MRW spectra. On raw finite samples, zeta alignment reduces
monofractal false positives, lowering calibrated fGn MRW compatibility from
about \(0.73\) to \(0.36\) in the documented alignment study. Nevertheless,
deterministic structure-function estimators and neural empirical-spectrum
estimators both show weak recovery of MRW curvature under short windows, with
projected \(\lambda^2\) nearly uncorrelated with the true intermittency
parameter in the tested finite-sample regime. These results indicate that
empirical spectrum estimation, rather than spectral interpretation alone, is the
central bottleneck in short-window MRW inference. CMIN-SR therefore provides a
calibrated diagnostic framework for multifractal spectral evidence, while
exposing a finite-sample identifiability limitation relevant to complex
stochastic and financial time series.

# 1. Introduction

Multiscale fluctuations are a defining feature of many complex systems. In
financial returns, turbulent-like cascades, and other stochastic signals,
observed paths may display heavy tails, clustered volatility, intermittent
bursts, and nonlinear scaling of empirical moments. Such behavior is often
described through multifractal spectra, where the scaling exponent
\(\zeta(q)\) varies nonlinearly with the moment order \(q\). In the
multifractal random walk (MRW) model, this nonlinearity is summarized by a
parabolic scaling spectrum controlled by a roughness parameter \(H\) and an
intermittency or curvature parameter \(\lambda^2\)
\cite{bacry2001multifractal,bacry2003log}. More generally, multifractal
formalism provides a language for connecting moment scaling, singularity
spectra, and cascade-like variability
\cite{halsey1986fractal,chhabra1989direct}.

The difficulty is that finite observations rarely provide clean access to the
underlying spectrum. Stable scaling may arise from monofractal processes such
as fractional Gaussian noise \cite{mandelbrot1968fractional}. Apparent
curvature may also be generated by heavy tails, volatility clustering, regime
changes, finite-size effects, or unstable high-order moments
\cite{cont2001stylized,bollerslev1986garch,hamilton1989regime,ludescher2011finite,grech2013spurious,bouchaud2000apparent}.
Consequently, estimating a nonzero spectral curvature from a finite sample is
not equivalent to establishing an MRW mechanism. This distinction is
particularly important in financial and other complex-system data, where
multifractal signatures can mix genuine multiscale dependence with distribution
tails, nonstationarity, and sampling artifacts
\cite{calvet2002multifractality,zhou2009components,barunik2012source}.

This paper studies MRW-like inference under finite samples as a diagnostic
problem rather than as a direct parameter prediction problem. We introduce
CMIN-SR, a calibrated multiscale spectral-representation framework that
separates empirical spectrum estimation from spectral geometry interpretation.
Given a finite path, the framework estimates an empirical spectrum
\(\zeta_{\mathrm{emp}}(q)\), projects it onto competing monofractal and MRW
spectral families, and reports diagnostic scores for scaling stability,
curvature, monofractal compatibility, MRW compatibility, boundary behavior, and
instability. The neural component is not treated as an unconstrained black-box
replacement for multifractal analysis; it is used as a finite-sample correction
and spectral-geometry calibrator whose outputs are interpreted through analytic
projection families and residuals.

The main contribution is therefore not a claim of guaranteed short-window
\(\lambda^2\) recovery. Instead, the contribution is a decomposition of
MRW-like spectral evidence into interpretable parts:

1. We formulate finite-sample MRW inference as validity-aware spectral
   diagnostics using empirical spectra, monofractal projections, MRW
   projections, residual geometry, and instability warnings.
2. We train and evaluate a spectrum-space calibrator that separates linear
   monofractal, boundary MRW, curved MRW, heavy-tail distorted, and
   regime-like spectra in controlled analytic zeta space.
3. We use raw zeta alignment and curvature-preserving alignment ablations to
   identify the trade-off between suppressing monofractal false curvature and
   preserving MRW curvature.
4. We perform an estimator-level finite-sample identifiability study showing
   that deterministic structure-function estimators also struggle to recover
   \(\lambda^2\) under the tested short-window settings.

Together, these results support a cautious conclusion: CMIN-SR is best viewed
as a validity-aware diagnostic framework for multifractal spectral evidence,
not as a guaranteed recovery engine for true MRW parameters from short noisy
samples.

# 2. Background and Problem Formulation

## 2.1 Empirical scaling spectra

Let \(x(t)\), \(t=1,\ldots,T\), denote a finite stochastic signal or increment
sequence. Multifractal analysis often studies the scaling of empirical
structure functions across scales \(a\) and moment orders \(q\). In a generic
form, one estimates quantities of the form
\[
S_q(a) \approx C_q a^{\zeta(q)},
\]
so that \(\zeta(q)\) can be estimated from log-log regressions of
\(\log S_q(a)\) against \(\log a\). The current repository implements
structure-function-style deterministic estimators, including ordinary least
squares, trimmed regression, bootstrap over scales, and smoothed zeta estimates
in `src/mrw_inverse/analysis/curvature_identifiability.py` and
`src/mrw_inverse/models/robust_zeta_estimator.py`.

The practical difficulty is that finite \(T\), limited scale sets, the chosen
q-grid, and high-order moment instability can all perturb
\(\zeta_{\mathrm{emp}}(q)\). In this paper, empirical spectra are therefore not
treated as direct evidence of a mechanism. They are first compared against
competing projection families.

## 2.2 Monofractal and MRW spectral families

A monofractal spectrum is represented as
\[
\zeta_{\mathrm{mono}}(q)=qH,
\]
where \(H\) is a scaling exponent. This linear family is an essential
competitor because a signal may show stable scaling while remaining
monofractal.

The MRW projection used in the repository follows the parabolic convention
\[
\zeta_{\mathrm{MRW}}(q)=qH-\frac{1}{2}\lambda^2 q(q-2),
\]
where \(H\) controls the leading linear scaling and \(\lambda^2\) controls
nonlinear spectral curvature. This formula is used as an analytic projection
family and decoder, not as proof that a sample was generated by an MRW process.
The projected parameter \(\lambda^2_{\mathrm{proj}}\) is therefore a coordinate
of the closest MRW-like spectral explanation under the chosen q-grid and
residual criterion.

## 2.3 Diagnostic inverse problem

The finite-sample inverse problem is
\[
x(t) \longrightarrow \zeta_{\mathrm{emp}}(q)
\longrightarrow \{H_{\mathrm{mono}},H_{\mathrm{proj}},
\lambda^2_{\mathrm{proj}}\}
\longrightarrow \text{diagnostics}.
\]
Unlike ordinary supervised regression, the goal is not merely to predict a
label or a parameter. The goal is to assess whether the finite empirical
spectrum provides stable evidence for a monofractal, boundary MRW, or curved
MRW-like interpretation. This is why projection residuals, curvature scores,
tail instability, and boundary behavior are reported together.

# 3. Proposed Method

## 3.1 Overview of the diagnostic framework

CMIN-SR is organized as a finite-sample spectral diagnostic pipeline:
\[
\text{finite path}
\rightarrow \text{empirical spectrum}
\rightarrow \text{monofractal/MRW projections}
\rightarrow \text{spectral geometry diagnostics}.
\]
The framework uses neural components only where they are constrained by the
spectral representation. In the final paper framing, the neural model should be
understood as a finite-sample correction and diagnostic calibrator, rather than
as an unconstrained black-box spectrum regressor.

## 3.2 Multiscale statistical representation

The empirical representation is based on a q-grid and scale set. The primary
q-grid used in the reported paper assets is
\[
q \in \{0.5,1.0,1.5,2.0,2.5,3.0\}.
\]
The deterministic identifiability study uses structure-function estimators over
finite scale sets and compares ordinary, trimmed, bootstrap, and smoothed
variants. The same q-grid is also used in analytic spectrum-space calibration
and in raw zeta alignment studies.

The current draft should avoid claiming a fully general estimator. The
implementation supports a concrete empirical-spectrum pipeline whose behavior
is evaluated under controlled synthetic settings and explicit q-grid/scale
sensitivity scripts.

## 3.3 Constrained parameter and projection inference

Given an empirical spectrum, the framework computes monofractal and MRW
projections. These projections produce:

- \(H_{\mathrm{mono}}\), the monofractal scaling coordinate;
- \(H_{\mathrm{proj}}\), the MRW linear coordinate;
- \(\lambda^2_{\mathrm{proj}}\), the MRW curvature coordinate;
- monofractal and MRW residual norms;
- MRW-vs-monofractal gain.

The projection coordinates are interpretable but conditional on the estimator,
q-grid, scale range, and projection family. This conditional interpretation is
central to the paper.

## 3.4 Analytic spectrum decoder

The analytic decoder maps the MRW projection coordinates back to a spectrum via
\[
\zeta_{\mathrm{MRW}}(q)=qH-\frac{1}{2}\lambda^2q(q-2).
\]
The monofractal decoder maps \(H\) to \(\zeta(q)=qH\). The repository contains
historical notes connecting these spectra to alpha and \(f(\alpha)\) geometry,
but the final paper assets do not currently include a confirmed
\(f(\alpha)\) reconstruction figure or quantitative table. Thus, in the main
draft, \(f(\alpha)\) should be mentioned as part of the multifractal background
and potential analytic decoding, but not claimed as a fully validated result
unless additional evidence is added.

## 3.5 Spectral geometry and consistency diagnostics

The spectral geometry calibrator receives spectrum-space features, projection
residuals, curvature diagnostics, and boundary scores, and outputs diagnostic
scores:

- \(p_{\mathrm{scaling}}\): stable scaling evidence;
- \(p_{\mathrm{curved}}\): nonlinear curvature evidence;
- \(p_{\mathrm{mono}}\): monofractal compatibility;
- \(p_{\mathrm{MRW}}\): MRW-compatible curved spectral evidence;
- \(p_{\mathrm{boundary}}\): low-curvature boundary behavior.

These are best described as calibrated diagnostic scores rather than fully
validated probabilistic calibration unless reliability diagrams or calibration
metrics are added. The calibrator is strongly supported in analytic spectrum
space, where linear, boundary, and curved spectra are separated cleanly.

# 4. Experiments

## 4.1 Experimental setup

The experimental evidence is based primarily on controlled synthetic processes
and analytic spectra. The process families include MRW, low-lambda MRW, fGn,
iid Gaussian, Student-t, GARCH(1,1), and regime-switching Gaussian controls.
The final paper assets emphasize:

- analytic spectrum-space calibration;
- controlled process-family diagnostics;
- projection residual geometry;
- finite-sample curvature identifiability;
- zeta noise bridge;
- analytic/deterministic/neural failure attribution.

Reported finite-sample settings include \(T=256,512,1024,2048\) in process
diagnostics and \(T=512,1024,2048\) in the quick deterministic identifiability
summary. Larger length and scale studies are implemented but should be reported
only according to available table evidence.

## 4.2 Baselines

Confirmed deterministic baselines include structure-function estimators:

- ordinary least-squares regression;
- trimmed regression;
- bootstrap over scales;
- smoothed zeta estimates.

These are used in the finite-sample identifiability study. The current
repository does not yet provide paper-ready MFDFA, WTMM, wavelet-leader, direct
neural spectrum regression, or unconstrained parameter-regression comparison
tables. These missing comparisons are important for CSF and are recorded in
`TODO_missing_evidence.md`.

## 4.3 Parameter estimation accuracy

The safest parameter-estimation result is negative and diagnostic. In the
deterministic identifiability table, lambda2 recovery is weak under the tested
finite-sample grid. At \(T=512\), lambda2 correlations are negative for all
implemented estimators. At \(T=1024\), the best reported correlation is
approximately \(0.136\), and at \(T=2048\) the correlations remain near zero or
negative. High-lambda detection rate is 0.0 in the quick diagnostic grid.

Therefore, the paper should not claim accurate recovery of true
\(\lambda^2\). The result should instead be interpreted as finite-sample
under-identifiability under the current q-grid, scale range, and estimator
family.

## 4.4 Spectrum reconstruction and spectral-geometry consistency

In analytic spectrum space, the spectral calibrator separates the intended
geometries. The table
`outputs/tables/spectral_geometry_calibrator_eval/summary_by_spectrum_type.csv`
reports:

- linear monofractal spectra:
  \(p_{\mathrm{curved}}\approx0.026\),
  \(p_{\mathrm{MRW}}\approx0.130\),
  \(p_{\mathrm{mono}}\approx0.969\);
- boundary MRW spectra:
  \(p_{\mathrm{MRW}}\approx0.352\);
- curved MRW spectra:
  \(p_{\mathrm{curved}}\approx0.888\),
  \(p_{\mathrm{MRW}}\approx0.935\),
  \(p_{\mathrm{mono}}\approx0.178\);
- heavy-tail distorted and regime-apparent spectra:
  low \(p_{\mathrm{MRW}}\) despite apparent instability or curvature.

This result supports the claim that spectral geometry interpretation is
learnable when the input spectrum is clean or controlled.

## 4.5 Finite-sample and noise robustness

The zeta noise bridge shows that controlled noise in zeta-space degrades
separation. The MRW-vs-linear \(p_{\mathrm{MRW}}\) separation margin decreases
from approximately 0.805 at zero noise to approximately 0.392 at noise level
0.1. This supports the interpretation that raw empirical zeta noise can be
large enough to alter the calibrated spectral interpretation.

The failure attribution study strengthens this conclusion. For high-lambda
MRW, the analytic spectrum gives \(p_{\mathrm{MRW}}\approx0.931\), while the
deterministic zeta estimate gives \(p_{\mathrm{MRW}}\approx0.142\), and the
neural zeta estimate gives \(p_{\mathrm{MRW}}\approx0.242\). Thus, the
calibrator recognizes clean MRW geometry, but both deterministic and neural
empirical spectra fail to preserve that curvature under the tested finite
samples.

## 4.6 Ablation study

Historical ablations are important but should be summarized rather than
over-expanded in the main text:

- CMIN-SR v1 over-associated stable scaling with MRW compatibility.
- CMIN-SR v2 introduced monofractal competition but also suppressed MRW scores.
- CMIN-SR v3 added an explicit curvature head, but fGn and MRW remained close
  in raw finite samples.
- Boundary calibration attempted same-H fGn/MRW contrastive calibration but did
  not solve the raw finite-sample ambiguity.
- Spectrum-space calibration succeeded, indicating that the interpretation
  layer was not the primary bottleneck.
- Raw zeta alignment suppressed fGn/Gaussian false curvature but over-smoothed
  true MRW curvature.
- Curvature-preserving zeta alignment exposed a trade-off rather than a final
  fix.

This ablation path supports the final interpretation: the bottleneck is
empirical spectrum estimation and finite-sample identifiability, not the lack
of additional validity heads.

# 5. Discussion

The experiments indicate that the core difficulty is not merely model capacity.
In clean spectrum space, the spectral geometry calibrator distinguishes linear,
boundary, and curved spectra. In finite raw samples, however, both deterministic
structure-function estimators and neural empirical-spectrum estimates may
flatten MRW curvature or create apparent monofractal curvature. This behavior
is consistent with known finite-size and spurious multifractality concerns in
empirical multifractal analysis.

The framework is therefore most useful when interpreted as a diagnostic
decomposition. A high scaling score means that scaling is stable, but not that
the process is multifractal. A nonzero MRW projection coordinate suggests a
curved spectral fit within the MRW family, but does not prove the underlying
mechanism. MRW compatibility should be read together with monofractal residuals,
MRW residuals, curvature scores, boundary scores, and tail-instability
warnings.

This perspective is particularly relevant for financial and other complex
systems, where heavy tails, volatility clustering, regime shifts, and finite
sampling can all affect high-order scaling estimates. CMIN-SR provides a way to
organize these effects into interpretable diagnostics. Its strongest result is
not a claim that short-window MRW parameters can always be recovered, but a
demonstration of where and why such recovery fails under the tested conditions.

# 6. Limitations

The present evidence is mainly synthetic and controlled. This is appropriate
for identifying estimator-level limitations, but it limits the strength of
claims about real-world complex systems. Real financial or physical data should
be treated as exploratory sanity checks unless additional validation is added.

The framework depends on the MRW parabolic projection family. This makes the
diagnostics interpretable, but it may be too restrictive for processes whose
spectra are multifractal but not MRW-like. The projected \(\lambda^2\) should
therefore be interpreted as an MRW-family coordinate rather than a universal
intermittency parameter.

Classical estimator comparisons are incomplete. The repository includes
structure-function deterministic estimators, but paper-ready MFDFA, WTMM,
wavelet-leader, and direct neural spectrum-regression baselines are not yet
available. A CSF submission would be stronger with at least one additional
classical multifractal baseline.

Finally, finite-sample identifiability remains difficult. The current q-grid,
scale range, and sample lengths do not reliably recover true \(\lambda^2\) in
the deterministic study. This is a central limitation, not a minor technical
detail.

# 7. Conclusion

This paper frames MRW-like inference from finite stochastic signals as a
validity-aware spectral diagnostic problem. CMIN-SR separates empirical
spectrum estimation from spectral interpretation, compares monofractal and MRW
projection families, and reports calibrated diagnostic scores for scaling,
curvature, monofractal compatibility, MRW compatibility, boundary behavior, and
instability.

The main scientific conclusion is that analytic spectral geometry is
interpretable and learnable, but raw finite-sample empirical spectrum
estimation is the bottleneck. Under the tested short-window settings,
deterministic estimators and neural zeta estimators both fail to recover MRW
curvature reliably. CMIN-SR should therefore be used as a calibrated diagnostic
framework for multifractal spectral evidence, not as a guaranteed
short-window MRW parameter recovery engine.
