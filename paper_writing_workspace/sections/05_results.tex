\section{Results}

\subsection{Analytic spectral geometry is stable and separable}

The clearest positive result is obtained in analytic spectrum space. Linear
monofractal spectra are assigned low curvature and low MRW compatibility,
boundary MRW spectra are assigned high boundary score and intermediate MRW
compatibility, and strongly curved MRW spectra are assigned high curvature and
high MRW compatibility. In the representative evaluation, linear monofractal
spectra have \(p_{\mathrm{curved}}\approx0.026\),
\(p_{\mathrm{MRW}}\approx0.130\), and
\(p_{\mathrm{mono}}\approx0.969\). Curved MRW spectra have
\(p_{\mathrm{curved}}\approx0.888\),
\(p_{\mathrm{MRW}}\approx0.935\), and
\(p_{\mathrm{mono}}\approx0.178\).

The three-seed supplement gives the same qualitative conclusion. Across seeds
2024, 2025, and 2026, curved MRW spectra have mean
\(p_{\mathrm{curved}}\approx0.893\), while linear monofractal spectra have
mean \(p_{\mathrm{curved}}\approx0.025\). This supports the claim that the
spectral interpretation layer learns the intended analytic geometry when the
input spectrum is controlled.

\subsection{Process-family controls show why diagnostics must be joint}

Raw process-family diagnostics show that no single score is sufficient.
Monofractal controls can have stable scaling; heavy-tailed and regime-like
controls can generate apparent curvature or instability; and GARCH-like
volatility clustering produces ambiguous behavior. These controls justify the
separation of scaling, curvature, monofractal compatibility, MRW compatibility,
boundary behavior, and instability.

The important point is not that the diagnostic labels every process perfectly.
The point is that it avoids a single ``stable scaling implies MRW'' rule. A
sample with high scaling evidence but low curvature evidence should not be
read as a strong MRW case. A sample with apparent curvature but high
instability should also be treated cautiously.

\subsection{Classical baselines also show finite-sample spectral error}

The supplemental classical baseline pass provides a useful reference point.
On the same quick finite-sample grid, first-order MFDFA has the lowest average
\(\lambda^2\) MAE among the added classical baselines and detects many
high-\(\lambda^2\) samples, but its \(\lambda^2\) correlation remains weak and
changes sign across sample lengths. Aggregated structure functions and compact
Haar wavelet-leader controls show near-zero or unstable correlation, while the
Haar WTMM-style maxima baseline degenerates to a low-curvature estimate on this
grid. These results strengthen the finite-sample conclusion rather than
showing that a single classical estimator solves short-window curvature
recovery.

\subsection{Finite-sample curvature recovery is weak in the tested grid}

The estimator-level study gives the central negative result. Across the tested
estimators, \(\lambda^2_{\mathrm{proj}}\) is only weakly related to the known
synthetic MRW curvature coordinate under short windows. In the
three-seed quick supplement, the mean \(\lambda^2\) correlation is close to
zero across \(T=512,1024,2048\), with estimator-dependent variation but no
stable recovery pattern. The high-\(\lambda^2\) detection rate remains weak in
the tested grid.

This result should be read carefully. It does not imply that MRW curvature is
never identifiable. It says that, under the current q-grid, scale range,
sample lengths, and estimator family, short-window empirical spectra do not
reliably preserve the curvature coordinate. This is the main reason the paper
frames CMIN-SR as a diagnostic framework rather than as a guaranteed
\(\lambda^2\) recovery engine.

\subsection{Zeta noise explains the raw-spectrum bottleneck}

The zeta-noise bridge shows that spectrum-space separation is sensitive to
input-spectrum noise. The MRW-vs-linear \(p_{\mathrm{MRW}}\) separation margin
is about \(0.804\) at zero noise in the three-seed supplement and decreases to
about \(0.365\) at noise level \(0.1\). The single refreshed run gives the
same qualitative pattern, with the margin falling from about \(0.805\) to
about \(0.338\). This is a direct explanation of why a successful analytic
calibrator can fail when its input \(\widehat{\zeta}(q)\) is distorted by
finite-sample estimation noise.

\subsection{Failure attribution localizes the bottleneck}

The failure-attribution study compares analytic, deterministic, and neural
spectra for the same process families. For high-\(\lambda^2\) MRW, the analytic
spectrum is correctly interpreted as MRW-compatible, with
\(p_{\mathrm{MRW}}\approx0.93\). The deterministic structure-function spectrum,
however, is interpreted as much less MRW-compatible, with
\(p_{\mathrm{MRW}}\approx0.13\) in the refreshed quick run. The neural
empirical spectrum improves the zeta MAE relative to the deterministic
estimate but still does not restore the analytic MRW curvature, giving
\(p_{\mathrm{MRW}}\approx0.33\).

This comparison is important because it separates a failure of spectral
interpretation from a failure of empirical spectrum estimation. The calibrator
recognizes clean MRW geometry. The difficulty is that short raw samples do not
reliably deliver that geometry to the calibrator.

\subsection{Ablations clarify the final interpretation}

The historical CMIN-SR sequence is best understood as a failure-analysis path,
not as a simple line of increasingly better models. Early versions
over-associated stable scaling with MRW compatibility. Later versions added
monofractal competition, explicit curvature diagnostics, boundary calibration,
and raw zeta alignment. These stages improved parts of the diagnostic behavior
but also exposed trade-offs. Raw zeta alignment reduced fGn and Gaussian false
MRW compatibility, but it also suppressed curvature in generated MRW samples.
Curvature-preserving alignment did not fully recover the lost curvature.

\subsection{Real-data sanity check}

As a cautious application check, the proxy diagnostic was applied to local
Fama-French factor returns. Original rolling windows were compared with fully
shuffled and block-shuffled surrogates for Mkt-RF, SMB, HML, and Mom. Under
full shuffling, the original-minus-surrogate proxy \(\lambda^2\) gaps are small
but positive: approximately \(0.0086\) for Mkt-RF, \(0.0129\) for SMB,
\(0.0100\) for HML, and \(0.0137\) for Mom. The corresponding MRW-diagnostic
gaps are also small and positive. Under block shuffling, the gaps are smaller,
near zero, or negative, indicating that the response is sensitive to how much
temporal structure the surrogate preserves.

These results are included only as a sanity check. They show that the
diagnostic responds to temporal structure that is partially destroyed by
shuffling. They do not demonstrate that financial factor returns are generated
by an MRW process.
